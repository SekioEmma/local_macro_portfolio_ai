# Market Risk Signals

This document stores abstract, non-private market-risk frameworks for local analyst memo answers. It must not contain raw conversations, account snapshots, real-time quotes, media excerpts, or institution forecasts.

## Market Top And Pullback-Risk Assessment

"Pullback risk rising" is a probabilistic judgment. It is not the same as a confirmed market top, and it is not the same as an imminent crash.

Use three layers when assessing claims that US equities have topped or that a large pullback is coming.

### Layer 1: Stage-Level Overheating Or Pullback Risk

Signals that can support a cautious pullback-risk framing include:

- elevated valuation
- rapid price appreciation
- crowded leading sectors
- rising long-end yields or real yields
- inflation or oil-price pressure
- weakening market breadth

This layer can justify language such as "risk has risen" or "margin of safety is lower." It does not by itself confirm a top.

### Layer 2: Medium-Term Trend Reversal

Signals that may support a stronger trend-reversal concern include:

- earnings expectations deteriorate
- liquidity conditions tighten materially
- credit spreads widen
- leadership narrows further or breaks down
- policy expectations turn materially restrictive

This layer requires more confirmation than a single valuation or rate signal. Avoid turning a stage-level warning into a trend-reversal claim.

### Layer 3: Systemic Crisis

Systemic-crisis language requires broad stress evidence, such as:

- credit and funding markets show stress
- banking or liquidity pressure emerges
- labor-market data weaken materially
- earnings revisions turn broadly negative
- volatility and cross-asset stress persist

Do not infer systemic crisis from equity weakness, expensive AI stocks, high yields, or gold/bond/equity co-movement alone. A safer framing is that systemic-crisis evidence is insufficient unless credit, funding, banking, employment, earnings, volatility, and liquidity signals confirm one another.

## Data Boundary

Do not quote current prices, PE ratios, yield levels, CPI, PPI, oil prices, credit spreads, FedWatch probabilities, or media claims unless the local context provides timestamped data. If the local context lacks them, say so and keep the answer at framework level.
